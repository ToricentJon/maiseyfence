Fencing in Eagle River

Eagle River sits in the Chugach Mountain foothills about 13 miles north of downtown Anchorage. While it's part of the Municipality of Anchorage, Eagle River has a distinctly different character — more rural, more wildlife, larger properties, and closer proximity to true wilderness.

Key considerations unique to Eagle River fencing:

  • Wildlife density — Eagle River has one of the highest moose densities in the Anchorage bowl; bears, coyotes, and even wolves are regular visitors from Chugach State Park
  • Larger lots — many Eagle River properties are 1-5+ acres, requiring cost-effective perimeter strategies
  • Wildland-urban interface — properties bordering state park land need fencing that balances containment with wildlife corridor access
  • River valley terrain — Eagle River valley lots often have significant slopes and uneven terrain requiring custom fence engineering
  • Snow load — Eagle River receives slightly more snow than Anchorage proper due to elevation and valley-effect precipitation
Living With Wildlife

Wildlife-Resistant Fencing

In Eagle River, your fence needs to coexist with Alaska's largest mammals. Here's what 30+ years has taught us.

Moose & Your Fence

Adult moose weigh 1,000-1,500 pounds and stand 6-7 feet at the shoulder. In Eagle River, they walk through neighborhoods daily — browsing on birch trees, garden vegetation, and ornamental plants. Your fence needs to account for them:

  • Visibility is key — moose walk through solid fences they can't see (especially cedar). Chain link and ornamental fences are visible and moose walk around them
  • Height matters — moose can step over 4-foot fences and lean over 5-foot fences to browse. 6-foot minimum is recommended in Eagle River
  • Cow-calf pairs in spring — mother moose are extremely protective and aggressive near fences where calves could get trapped. Keep bottom clearance consistent
  • Rut season (September-October) — bull moose become aggressive and may charge fences; heavy-gauge materials resist impact better

Bear, Coyote & Other Wildlife

Eagle River's proximity to Chugach State Park means other wildlife interactions too:

  • Black bears — attracted to garbage, bird feeders, and gardens. A 6-foot chain link or solid fence deters most bears. Critical: don't create enclosed spaces where bears can't escape — they'll panic and cause more damage
  • Coyotes — increasing in Eagle River neighborhoods. A 6-foot fence with no gaps at the bottom contains pets and excludes coyotes. Coyotes can squeeze through 4-inch gaps
  • Lynx and fox — occasional visitors; standard fencing is adequate. Avoid creating entrapment hazards for smaller wildlife

We design fences that protect your property while respecting wildlife corridors. In Eagle River, fencing is about coexistence, not exclusion.

Big Lots, Smart Solutions

Large Property Fencing

Fencing 2-5+ acres of Eagle River property entirely in cedar would cost a fortune. Here's the smart approach.

1

Perimeter Chain Link

Run affordable chain link around your full property boundary. At $15-$35/foot, this is the most cost-effective way to establish a defined perimeter. Chain link is also visible to moose, reducing wildlife damage.

2

Privacy Zone Cedar

Install premium cedar fencing around the immediate yard area near your house — typically 50-100 feet of the property closest to your home. This gives you privacy where you actually spend time, at a fraction of full-lot cost.

3

Strategic Gates

Place walk gates and driveway gates at key access points. Every gate includes our Maisey Latch so it continues working despite Eagle River's freeze-thaw ground movement.

4

Future Expansion

We design the perimeter framework to easily upgrade sections later. As your budget allows, you can replace chain link sections with cedar or ornamental panels over time.
