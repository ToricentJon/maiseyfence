The Problem

Why Standard Gate Latches Fail in Alaska

Every fence contractor in Alaska knows the frustration: you install a beautiful gate, the latch works perfectly — and six months later the customer calls because it won't close. The gate is fine. The posts are fine. The ground moved.

Alaska's freeze-thaw cycles push fence posts up and down by 1-3 inches every season. Standard gate latches are rigid — they demand exact alignment between the gate and the post. When the ground heaves or settles even a fraction of an inch, the strike plate and latch bolt no longer line up. The gate won't close, or worse, it won't open.

Homeowners end up with gates propped open with bungee cords, dogs getting out, property unsecured — all because of a $15 latch that wasn't designed for the real world.

We got tired of the callbacks. So we engineered a solution.

The Solution

Introducing the Maisey Latch

The Maisey Latch uses a patent-pending channel system instead of a fixed strike plate. The latch striker rides in a vertical channel that allows up to 3 inches of vertical movement — accommodating frost heave, ground settling, and seasonal post shifting without ever losing engagement.

Key engineering features:

  • Vertical channel system — the striker moves freely up and down while maintaining full latch engagement at every position
  • Heavy-gauge steel construction — built from thick-gauge galvanized steel that won't bend, warp, or corrode in Alaska's salt air and sub-zero temperatures
  • One-hand operation — easy to open with gloves, mittens, or cold hands — critical in Alaska where you're bundled up 8 months a year
  • Self-adjusting — no seasonal adjustments needed. Install it once and forget it. The channel does the work year after year
  • Universal mounting — compatible with cedar, chain link, ornamental, and vinyl gates of all sizes
